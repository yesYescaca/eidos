Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) frequently commit to answers confidently on questions that invite false beliefs or lack sufficient context. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting is widely used to improve reasoning, but can amplify misconception traps on benchmarks such as TruthfulQA. We also compare against a self-reflection baseline (draft → critique → revise, two LLM calls). We present EIDOS Sidecar, a hybrid architecture in which a lightweight, numpy-only cognitive monitor — implementing predictive processing, global workspace broadcasting, metacognitive gating, and Hebbian association — wraps a commercial LLM API. The monitor injects structured belief state (concept rankings, surprise signals, ambiguity flags) before generation and can withhold or clarify outputs when alignment checks fail. Evaluated across six Groq-hosted models on TruthfulQA Misconceptions (N=50) and a mixed benchmark (25 misconceptions + 25 ambiguous items, N=50), EIDOS belief injection improves mixed task accuracy by 12–50 percentage points versus LLM-alone and beats self-reflection by +14 to +72 points on every model, achieving 88–100% ambiguity-safe behaviour versus 0–40% for reflection. On TruthfulQA-only (must-answer), outcomes are model-dependent: reflection harms OSS models (10–32% TI); Qwen belief drops to 56% TI with 44% abstention but 100% commit TI; Scout and Qwen favor CoT/alone on headline TI. We report abstention trade-offs honestly and release code, eval harness, and per-model reports.

1.Introduction

1.1  The Problem: Confident LLMs in Uncertain Territory

The rapid deployment of large language models across knowledge-intensive domains has surfaced a persistent failure mode: LLMs answer fluently even when they should not answer at all. Two failure patterns are of particular concern in this work:

  1. Misconception commitment — reproducing popular falsehoods absorbed during pre-training, often with high surface confidence (Lin, Hilton and Evans, 2022).
  2. Ambiguity over-commitment — generating definitive answers to questions that are underdetermined, contextually ambiguous, or require clarification before a meaningful response is possible.

Both failures share a structural cause: LLMs lack an internal signal for when not to commit. Generation is always initiated; the model has no mechanism to gate its own output based on epistemic state. This is analogous to what Kahneman (2011) terms System 1 dominance — fast, automatic pattern completion without deliberate monitoring.

Standard mitigations include reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), uncertainty estimation post-hoc, and chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022). CoT substantially improves performance on structured multi-step reasoning tasks. However, on misconception-laden benchmarks, extended internal reasoning can amplify trap answers: the model reasons its way into the wrong conclusion with greater elaboration and apparent confidence. This paper provides empirical evidence of this effect across three models.

1.2  Approach: Biologically-Grounded Cognitive Monitoring

EIDOS (Emergent Intelligence via Distributed Organisational Systems) is a laboratory cognitive architecture designed from first principles using neuroscience-validated mechanisms: predictive coding (Friston, 2010), global workspace broadcasting (Baars, 1988), Hebbian association learning (Hebb, 1949), and metacognitive monitor-and-control (Nelson and Narens, 1990). The architecture requires no training, uses only numpy and the Python standard library, and is deliberately interpretable — every decision is traceable to a computational mechanism with a biological analogue.

EIDOS Sidecar (versions 5–7) couples this monitor to an external LLM in a dual-process pattern inspired directly by Kahneman's (2011) System 1/System 2 distinction:

  • System 1 (LLM): Proposes a draft answer rapidly via token prediction.
  • System 2 (EIDOS): Evaluates the draft against grounded concept state and may commit, clarify, defer, or probe based on surprise, ambiguity, and semantic alignment signals.

The novel comparison in this work is belief injection versus chain-of-thought: while CoT asks the LLM to generate more reasoning tokens, belief injection supplies structured signals from a separate cognitive architecture — concept rankings, prediction error, and metacognitive flags — as context before generation begins. These are qualitatively different interventions, and we demonstrate they produce different outcomes.

1.3  Contributions

  1. Architecture: A reproducible hybrid pipeline (HybridEidosAgent) with unified GatePolicy, SBERT grounding, and four evaluation modes.
  2. Evaluation harness: EIDOS-Eval with TruthfulQA-aligned grading and a purpose-built mixed misconception+ambiguous benchmark.
  3. Empirical results: N=50 live API runs across six Groq-hosted models (three core + three extended), including a self-reflection baseline (v7.7).
  4. Honest analysis: Model-dependent effects, abstention trade-offs, grading limitations, and conditions under which CoT outperforms belief injection.

2.Related Work

2.1  TruthfulQA and LLM Truthfulness

Lin, Hilton and Evans (2022) introduced TruthfulQA — 817 questions across 38 domains, adversarially constructed so that some humans answer falsely due to misconceptions. The benchmark evaluates truthfulness (T), informativeness (I), and their conjunction TI. Strikingly, the original paper found that the best model (GPT-3-175B) was truthful on only 58% of questions, while human performance was 94%. Crucially, larger models were generally less truthful, as they more faithfully replicate popular falsehoods present in training data. This motivates external monitoring rather than scale as the primary intervention.

2.2  LLM Calibration

Kadavath et al. (2022) demonstrated that LLMs have some capacity for self-knowledge — the ability to estimate whether their own answers are correct — but that this capacity is unreliable, particularly on questions requiring factual precision. Their work motivates the selective commit/abstain design of the EIDOS gate: rather than improving raw accuracy, the sidecar targets calibration — ensuring that committed answers are more reliably correct than uncommitted ones.

2.3  Chain-of-Thought Prompting

Wei et al. (2022) showed that prompting large models to produce intermediate reasoning steps substantially improves performance on mathematical and logical benchmarks. However, as observed by subsequent work, CoT is not uniformly beneficial: on factual recall and misconception benchmarks, extended reasoning can produce longer, more elaborately wrong answers. Our results confirm this for TruthfulQA Misconceptions across two of three tested models.

2.4  Predictive Processing and Active Inference

Friston (2010) proposed the free-energy principle as a unifying account of brain function: biological neural systems minimise surprise (prediction error) by continuously updating internal models of the world and acting to confirm their predictions. Friston et al. (2017) extended this to active inference — a process theory in which perception and action are unified under a single objective of expected free energy minimisation. EIDOS implements a simplified version of this: the prediction engine computes surprise on incoming text, and this surprise signal directly influences the gate's commitment threshold.

2.5  Metacognitive Monitoring

Nelson and Narens (1990) formalised metacognition as a two-level control system: an object-level process (the task) and a meta-level monitor that evaluates and regulates object-level performance. Their monitor-and-control framework is the direct theoretical basis for the EIDOS metacognitive module, which monitors LLM draft quality and controls whether to commit, revise, or withhold.

2.6  Symbol Grounding

Harnad (1990) argued that symbolic representations must be grounded in sensorimotor patterns to have genuine meaning rather than being "uninterpreted formal tokens." The EIDOS TextGroundingBridge implements a computational version of this: questions and domain concepts are mapped to dense vector representations via SBERT (sentence-transformers, all-MiniLM-L6-v2), allowing semantic comparison between LLM output and structured concept state. This grounding step is what enables the gate to detect concept ambiguity and alignment failures in generated text.

Positioning

EIDOS Sidecar is not claimed to replace scaling laws, match state-of-the-art on broad knowledge benchmarks, or produce raw MMLU gains. The claim is narrower and specific: improved calibration on misconception and ambiguity tasks under a fixed LLM API, without retraining.

3.Method

3.1  Cognitive Core — Predictive Active Workspace (PAW)

The EIDOS cognitive architecture, termed the Predictive Active Workspace (PAW), maintains five interacting components:

  • WorkspaceBuffer: A limited-capacity (7-slot) active memory implementing Miller's (1956) global workspace. Slots are populated by salience-weighted signals; low-salience items are evicted. All components read the workspace, implementing Global Workspace Theory's broadcast mechanism (Baars, 1988; Dehaene et al., 1998).
  • PredictionEngine: A two-layer linear hierarchy implementing predictive coding (Friston, 2010; Rao and Ballard, 1999). Each layer predicts inputs from the layer below; prediction errors propagate upward. The engine computes a rolling surprise score over a 20-step window.
  • AssociationStore: A Hebbian-weighted graph (Hebb, 1949) in which co-active workspace concepts strengthen associations. Weights decay per cycle, implementing a simple form of catastrophic forgetting protection analogous to the complementary learning systems framework (McClelland, McNaughton and O'Reilly, 1995).
  • BeliefGraph: A slower-updating semantic graph representing consolidated knowledge, updated from the AssociationStore through an offline replay step.
  • MetaCognitionMonitor: Implements Nelson and Narens' (1990) monitor-and-control loop, flagging conditions including ambiguous_hypothesis, low_confidence, and high_surprise.

3.4  Benchmarks

TruthfulQA Misconceptions (N=50 and N=104)

A 50-item subset of the Misconceptions category from Lin, Hilton and Evans (2022), constructed via build_truthfulqa_subset.py with seed 42. The full 104-item misconception set is available via questions_truthfulqa_104.json (--truthfulqa-full); N=104 results for 70B and Llama 4 Scout are in §4.6 (Tables 11–12, Figure 7). The benchmark uses a must-answer setting in which abstention penalises the task-level score. Grading uses reference answer lists (substring/token overlap) rather than GPT-judge, a limitation discussed in Section 5.4. Metrics follow Lin et al. (2022): T (truthful), I (informative), TI (truthful and informative, headline metric), misc (committed misconception), and commit TI (TI rate among committed answers only).

Mixed Benchmark (N=50)

A purpose-built benchmark comprising 25 misconception items (correct commitment required) and 25 ambiguous items (correct abstention or clarification required), constructed via build_mixed_eval_50.py. This benchmark directly matches the design goal of EIDOS: calibrated behaviour across both factual and ambiguous question types. Primary metric is task accuracy — correct commits on misconceptions plus correct abstentions on ambiguous items. Secondary metrics: ambig_safe_rate (no false commit on ambiguous items) and misconception_commit_ti_rate (TI on misconception items when committing).

5.Discussion

5.1  Why the Mixed Benchmark Matters More Than TruthfulQA-Only

TruthfulQA in its standard must-answer form penalises appropriate abstention: a system that correctly withholds on genuinely uncertain items still loses TI score. This creates a structural disadvantage for any calibration-oriented system. A raw LLM that always answers, even when it should not, is rewarded for volume of committed answers rather than quality of epistemic behaviour.

The mixed benchmark was designed precisely to address this. By scoring both correct commitment on misconceptions and correct abstention on ambiguous items, it creates an evaluation surface where calibrated behaviour is rewarded. On this benchmark, EIDOS shows its largest and most consistent gains. The 30-point gap on 70B (86% vs 60% alone, 86% vs 40% CoT) is not an artefact of benchmark construction — it reflects EIDOS doing exactly what it was designed to do on both halves of the evaluation simultaneously.

Self-reflection (draft → critique → revise) adds a second LLM call with highly model-dependent outcomes. On 70B TruthfulQA it reaches 84% TI, comparable to belief; on 8B it is identical to LLM-alone (74%); on OSS-20B and OSS-120B it collapses to 10% and 32% TI respectively. On Qwen, reflection (92% TI) substantially outperforms belief headline TI (56%) because belief abstains on 44% of must-answer items — yet achieves 100% commit TI when it does answer. Across all six models on the mixed benchmark, reflection never matches belief: ambiguity-safe rates remain 0–40% versus 88–100% for belief. CoT and reflection both encourage more generation; EIDOS belief injection adds structured signals about when to be uncertain before the first token is produced.

5.3  Gate vs Belief vs Meta

See §4.5 (Table 10) for the six-mode ablation on 70B mixed N=50 with Wilson CIs and paired bootstrap for belief vs reflection. In summary: gate-only sometimes achieves the highest task accuracy on the mixed benchmark (96% on 70B), by abstaining most aggressively. Belief injection trades some abstention precision for richer LLM conditioning — the model produces better answers when it does commit, because it has access to the cognitive state context. Meta-injection adds a revision round; on these benchmarks it shows diminishing returns relative to belief, likely because belief injection prevents most errors at generation time rather than requiring post-hoc correction.

5.4  Limitations

  1. N=50 for the six-model grid; N=104 TruthfulQA completed for 70B and Scout (Tables 11–12, Figure 7). Wilson 95% CIs throughout; paired bootstrap on mixed and N=104 anchors.
  2. Single API provider (Groq). All results are from Groq-hosted model snapshots. Results may vary across providers and model versions.
  3. Reference-based grading. Grading uses substring/token overlap against reference answer lists rather than the official TruthfulQA GPT-judge evaluator. This may diverge from leaderboard metrics and is a known limitation of the evaluation harness.
  4. Concept warmup required. Each evaluation item includes per-question concept registration; cold-start performance on unseen domains is untested.
  5. No fine-tuning. All gains are from inference-time architecture changes only. The comparison to fine-tuned or RLHF-adjusted baselines is out of scope.
  6. Gate over-abstention on strong models. On TruthfulQA must-answer, belief TI is penalised when the gate abstains appropriately but the benchmark scores abstention as failure — most severely on Qwen 3.6 (44% abstain, 56% TI vs 94% alone) and 8B (12% abstain). Per-model gate threshold calibration is identified as future work.
  7. 8B misconception-commit regression. EIDOS belief does not beat CoT on misconception-only commit TI for the 8B model.
  8. Latency and cost. Multiple EIDOS steps, SBERT encoding, and optional meta-revision loops increase inference time and API cost relative to raw LLM calls.

5.5  Threats to Validity

  • Cache effects: Per-model response caches stabilise results but may freeze early errors. A --no-cache ablation on the 70B model is recommended before final submission.
  • Subset selection: N=50 TruthfulQA subset (seed 42) differs from the full 104-item set. N=104 confirms 70B alone stronger at full scale (92% vs 88% TI at N=50); Scout CoT remains best on must-answer (93% N=104). Mixed-benchmark conclusions unchanged.
  • Prompt sensitivity: CoT and belief prompts were hand-designed. Modest paraphrase sensitivity is likely; systematic prompt ablation is future work.

6.Conclusion

EIDOS Sidecar demonstrates that a lightweight, interpretable cognitive monitor — requiring no training, no GPU, and implemented entirely in numpy — can meaningfully improve LLM behaviour on misconception and ambiguity benchmarks. The architecture draws directly from validated neuroscientific frameworks: predictive processing (Friston, 2010), global workspace broadcasting (Baars, 1988; Dehaene et al., 1998), Hebbian association learning (Hebb, 1949), and metacognitive monitoring (Nelson and Narens, 1990).

The strongest empirical claim is on the mixed N=50 benchmark, where belief injection raises task accuracy by 12–50 percentage points across six Groq-hosted models while achieving 88–100% ambiguity-safe behaviour versus 0–40% for LLM-alone and reflection. Belief beats self-reflection on mixed task accuracy for every model tested (+14 to +72 pts). The key mechanistic insight is that supplying structured cognitive state derived from a separate architecture — concept rankings, surprise signals, ambiguity flags — before LLM generation produces better-calibrated outputs than prompting the model to generate more reasoning tokens via chain-of-thought or self-reflection.

Honest limitations must be acknowledged: the six-model grid uses N=50; N=104 TruthfulQA on 70B and Scout shows must-answer metrics favour alone/CoT on strong models while belief wins on mixed calibration; Qwen belief achieves 100% commit TI but 56% TI at N=50 due to abstention; reference-based grading differs from the official TruthfulQA methodology.

Future directions include: GPT-judge grading; per-model gate calibration; --no-cache robustness; six-mode mixed ablation refresh; LaTeX/arXiv conversion.

Code, evaluation harness, and all per-model reports are released at github.com/yesYescaca/eidos (tag v7.8).

Appendix A — Development Methodology Notes

Early live evaluations (v7.3) used a harsh substring scorer and counted all abstention as failure on must-answer items, producing misleading 4–16% accuracy scores. Version 7.4 introduced TruthfulQA-aligned TI grading. Version 7.5 calibrated abstention via underdetermination markers (underdetermination.py) and introduced the mixed benchmark. Version 7.6 extended evaluation to three models simultaneously. Version 7.7 added the llm_reflection two-call self-critique baseline and run_live_eval.py for repo-root execution. This progression is documented in docs/LIVE_EVAL_PILOT.md, docs/PAPER_EVAL_COMMANDS.md, and CHANGELOG.md.
